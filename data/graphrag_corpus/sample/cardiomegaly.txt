Cardiomegaly and Heart Failure — Clinical Notes (sample, open text)

Cardiomegaly denotes an enlarged cardiac silhouette on the chest radiograph, commonly quantified by a
cardiothoracic ratio greater than 0.5 on a posteroanterior film. It is frequently associated with
congestive heart failure. Elevated B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) supports a cardiac cause of
dyspnoea and pulmonary oedema. Loop diuretics such as furosemide are first-line for fluid overload in
decompensated heart failure. Pleural effusions, often bilateral, accompany cardiac decompensation and
may be seen together with cardiomegaly and pulmonary vascular congestion on imaging.

Differential considerations for an enlarged cardiac silhouette include true chamber enlargement,
pericardial effusion, and technical factors such as a low-volume or anteroposterior projection. Normal
BNP makes a primary cardiac cause of acute dyspnoea less likely and shifts the differential toward
pulmonary or infective processes.

Pneumonia, Pleural Effusion, and Bilateral Opacities — Clinical Notes (sample, open text)

Pneumonia presents on chest radiography as airspace consolidation, often lobar, sometimes with air
bronchograms. An elevated white blood cell count (leukocytosis) supports an infective process, whereas
a normal white cell count makes bacterial pneumonia less likely. Atelectasis (collapse) can mimic
consolidation but typically shows volume loss and fissural displacement.

Bilateral opacities have a broad differential. In a patient with elevated BNP and bilateral opacities,
cardiogenic pulmonary oedema is favoured; with fever and leukocytosis, multifocal pneumonia or acute
respiratory distress is considered. Pleural effusion appears as blunting of the costophrenic angle and
a meniscus; large effusions cause mediastinal shift. Pneumothorax, by contrast, shows a visible
visceral pleural line with absent lung markings peripherally and can be life-threatening under tension.

These distinctions matter for management: antibiotics for pneumonia, diuresis for cardiogenic oedema,
and drainage for large effusions or tension pneumothorax.

This Policy sets out the general principles that underpin the University's approach to research ethics.

### 1.1 DEFINITION

Research ethics is the protection of human and animal subjects in the planning, conduct and reporting of research (Resources for Research Ethics Education, 2016).

### 1.2 RESEARCHER RESPONSIBILITY

It is the role of Academic Staff to familiarise themselves and comply with this Policy, its associated procedures and applicable School research ethics-related policies and practices.

It is the responsibility of Academic staff or Principal Researcher to ensure compliance with the Policy.

In the case of Student-led research, it is the responsibility of programme and/or course leaders, research project supervisors, to make students aware of the Policy. However, it is the responsibility that Student researchers follow School research guidelines and declare where necessary conformance to codes of ethical conduct.

The University Research Ethics Policy provides a general framework for School research ethics practice at Heriot-Watt University, including the Animal Ethics Committee and the Genetic Modification Committee.

The University is committed to best practice in relation to research ethics as it ensures research is conducted in a manner that respects the dignity, rights and welfare of participants.

Research ethics are taken seriously by the University to minimise risk to participants, researchers, third parties and the University itself.

## 5. OBJECTIVES

### 5.1 ETHICAL PRINCIPLES

The Policy recognises and advocates the use of the following ethical principles:

#### a. Prevention of Harm

Academic Staff and Students must seek to protect participants from physical and psychological harm during the research process. Researchers should not make frivolous use of participants. Researchers must also take steps to protect their own physical and psychological well-being during the research process. The risk of harm must reflect a balance of impact on participants and the benefits of the research.

#### b. Informed Consent

Informed consent helps to minimise harm to participants. Without informed consent, participants may feel manipulated, humiliated or mistreated by researchers. Full participant consent is necessary unless there is a strong rationale for no or partial consent. Consent should be attained by researchers informing participants in advance of all necessary information expected to influence willingness to take part in the study. Participants should be given the opportunity to ask questions before securing consent. For longitudinal research methods, approval may need to be sought at more than one juncture of the study.

#### c. Rights of Participants

In giving consent, participants retain the right to withdraw this consent. Researchers should indicate at what point in the study participants can withdraw consent or request data destruction. Participants should be informed of what measures are in place for consent withdrawal.

#### d. Minimising Risk with Vulnerable Participants

Some participants should automatically be considered vulnerable due to limited ability to provide consent, e.g., young children, people who are ill or bereaved. Other groups may be vulnerable due to context, e.g., unemployed, migrants, refugees. Extra safeguards and consent procedures must be designed when recruiting vulnerable participants.

#### e. Respect for Participants

Researchers should aim to conduct research that is respectful of national and international law, gender differences, all groups in society, and marginalised/disadvantaged groups.

#### f. Confidentiality

Unless agreed otherwise, research findings should be communicated in a manner that protects participant confidentiality. Researchers must protect the confidentiality of participant identity and data throughout the research project.

#### g. Appropriate Use of Rewards and Incentives

Incentivising participation should only be on the basis of making people want to take part, rather than making participation dependent on the reward or inability to refuse.

#### h. Anti-discriminatory

Researchers should act in a manner that complies with the Equality Act 2010.

### 5.2 ETHICAL CONDUCT

The Policy also recognises and advocates the following principles in relation to academic conduct:

#### a. Reciprocity

Research should be based on the creation of outcomes for the common good.

#### b. Accessibility

Researchers should aim wherever possible to disseminate their findings in the public domain and through learning and teaching roles at the University.

#### c. Independence

Researchers should not distort research design and/or findings to suit funder requirements.

#### d. Specified Use of Research Funding

Researchers must not use funding for purposes other than that specified in their grant award.

#### e. Safe and Secure Data Management

Steps must be taken to retain all research materials gathered (including physical and visual data) in a safe and confidential space for a minimum period of five years. Where data retention is long-term, store in electronic format on a University server with password protection. Participants should be informed about data management and retention through informed consent.

#### f. Three Rs

Research involving animals should aim to conform to the principles of replacement, reduction and refinement.

#### g. Ethical Bioprospecting

Researching the commercial use of natural resources must be respectful of indigenous territories and cultures, and take account of relevant international agreements (e.g., Nagoya Protocol).

#### h. Conform to the Universal Declaration of Bioethics and Human Rights

Researchers should subscribe to universal guidelines covering all issues in the field of bioethics.

The University does not automatically disbar security-sensitive research. Materials engaging Terrorism Act (2006) provisions should be kept on specially designated University servers, not personal equipment, allowing access to authorised researchers only.

All research requires ethical review and, if deemed necessary, formal ethical approval should be sought. Further ethical approval or re-approval may be required should significant details change. **Note: retrospective ethical approval cannot be granted.**

All pilot research is subject to ethical approval. Ethical approval is a devolved matter and responsibility of School research ethics committees.

Research ethics-related misconduct by researchers is covered by the University's Disciplinary Code and Procedures for Investigating Research Misconduct.

Research ethics-related misconduct by Students is covered by Regulation 50 of the University Regulations and the Student Discipline Policy and Procedure.

Examples of research ethics-related misconduct include:
- Misappropriation of another's intellectual property by plagiarism or breach of confidence
- Misrepresentation of research findings by deception or lying
- Obstruction, including withholding, destroying or falsifying evidence
- Unfairly influencing witnesses or interviewees
- Breach of confidentiality required by external contracts
- Deliberate commercial exploitation of ideas of others without acknowledgement
- Failing to comply with statutory or institutional regulations

## 2. RESEARCH ETHICS PROCEDURES

### 2.1 SCOPE

The Research Ethics Procedures:

- Provide a framework for the conduct of ethical procedures for School committees
- Sit within the broader framework of research integrity and values of the University
- Conform with all related legislation
- Consistent with research ethics procedures of key external institutions

### 2.2 PURPOSE

The purposes of these Procedures are to:

- Provide recommended guidelines for committees on good research ethics practice
- Harmonise research ethics procedures across the University
- Complement research ethics procedures of key external/international institutions
- Link research ethics to the University's Strategic Plan

### 2.3 WHAT IS ETHICAL REVIEW/APPROVAL?

The University differentiates between:

#### a) Ethical Review

Assesses whether ethical implications are present (study involves human participants, animals, personal data from external sources, or raises other ethical issues).

Schools may adopt different approaches for different groups (staff, postgraduate students, undergraduate students).

#### b) Ethical Approval

Involves rigorous assessment of ethical implications and researchers' plans regarding adherence to ethical principles and legislation. Types include:

**1) "In Principle Approval"** – Must be obtained before applying for funding through RMAS/Worktribe. Granted after light-touch review to ensure initial research plans are defensible on ethical grounds.

**2) "Full Approval"** – Must be obtained before data collection commences. Granted after rigorous assessment of applicants' plans regarding ethical risks, mitigations, and conduct.

**Important:** A project approved in principle must still obtain full approval before data collection. Failure to obtain full ethical approval may be considered academic misconduct.

### 2.4 WHEN SHOULD ETHICAL APPROVAL BE SOUGHT?

- **In Principle Approval:** Applied for when overall concept, goals and methodology are agreed
- **Full Approval:** Must be obtained before primary data collection commences

For external funding applications:
- Either "in-principle" or "full" approval must be obtained before submission to RMAS/Worktribe
- The Research Office will not sign off a project until at least "in-principle" approval is obtained

**Important:** Full ethical approval for student research projects must be obtained even if unfunded or not recorded in RMAS/Worktribe, if the ethical review indicates this is required.

### 2.5 WHO SHOULD APPLY FOR ETHICAL APPROVAL?

For **staff-led research:** The Principal Investigator is responsible for gaining approval.

For **student-led research:** Programme/course leaders and research project supervisors must make students aware of the Policy. **Student researchers are responsible for following School guidelines** and declaring conformance to codes of ethical conduct.

### 2.6 HOW TO APPLY FOR ETHICAL APPROVAL?

#### 2.6.1 Research conducted by staff

If ethical approval is required, obtain it through the university's online Ethics Management System (EMS).

- EMS applications are assessed and approval granted by the School Research Ethics Committee
- Changes to an application may be required before approval
- EMS applications involving animals are directed to the Animal Welfare Ethical Review Body (AWERB)
- EMS integrates data protection issue assessment
- If data protection risks are deemed high, complete a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA), reviewed by the University's Data Protection Officer (DPO)

**Timeline:** Allow at least 10 working days (depending on School requirements) for review. Process may take longer if changes are requested or DPIA is required.

**Next steps:**
- Once approval letter is received from School Ethics Committee via EMS, upload it to RMAS/Worktribe tagged as 'ethics'
- For awarded grants, provide PDF copy of 'in principle' document to Research Grants Finance Team before grant setup
- 'Full approval' can only be granted for specific data collection activities with full detail

#### 2.6.2 Research conducted by students

For student-led research requiring full approval, obtain via EMS.

- Applications can be shared between student and supervisor for collaborative editing
- Supervisors must digitally sign before student submission
- A letter confirming approval/rejection is automatically generated and emailed to students

### 2.7 DATA PROTECTION CONSIDERATIONS

Under GDPR and other legislation, the University must embed data protection by design and default into all research involving personal data. Projects with potentially high privacy risks require a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA).

The Ethics Management System requires applicants to complete a DPIA if screening indicates necessity, in accordance with University Data Protection Policy.

### 2.8 ADDITIONAL RESEARCH APPROVAL

Researchers must check whether approval from other relevant research authorities is necessary, including:

- **NHS Research Ethics Committee** approval if the study involves:
  - NHS patients (including deceased patients) and their relatives
  - NHS staff
  - NHS premises

# Working with Users and Ethics

**Course:** F21RP - Research Methods and Project Planning

---

## Overview

Research involving users and data collection requires careful consideration of ethical principles, data protection, and proper methodological approaches.

---

## Research Data Types

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Data

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Qualitative Data** | Subjective; what the user is thinking, experiencing | Open-ended interview responses, observations, textual feedback |
| **Quantitative Data** | Objective; measured by the researcher | Surveys, Likert scales, metrics (time, number of clicks), questionnaires |

---

## Ways to Work with Users

The following methods are commonly used for collecting user data:

- **Focus Groups** – Group discussions about your product/research
- **Interviews** – One-on-one discussions with users
- **Surveys** – Standardised questionnaires (SUS, SUMI, PSSUQ)
- **Prototyping** – Testing working prototypes with users
- **User Experience (UX) Testing** – Observing users interacting with a system
- **Expert Consultation** – Feedback from domain experts
- **Crowdsourcing** – Gathering data from large distributed populations

---

## How Many Users Do You Need?

### Formative Evaluation (User-Centred Design)

**Purpose:** Find out what users think before software is finished

- **Focus:** Exploratory; requires depth not many users
- **Participants:** Typically 5-10 users
- **Goal:** Understand user needs, preferences, pain points

### Summative Evaluation

**Purpose:** Hypothesis testing on final product (e.g., system A vs. system B)

- **Focus:** Requires statistical significance
- **Participants:** Often 40+ for statistical validity
- **Goal:** Prove effectiveness, compare systems

⚠️ **Important:** Do NOT present statistics on small numbers and claim significance

---

## Survey and Question Types

### Binary Questions

Two possible answers (Yes/No)

- **Avoid in general** — consider offering a third option
- Better options: "Don't know," "Not applicable," "Other"

### Closed Questions

Specific factual information; ask participants to pick from a list

Example:
- How often do you use this feature? [Daily / Weekly / Monthly / Never]

### Likert Scale

Rate agreement or satisfaction on a scale (typically 1-5)

Example:
- **"This system is easy to use"**: [1 = Strongly Disagree ... 5 = Strongly Agree]

### Open-Ended Questions

Invite participant description; useful to avoid bias but harder to process

Example:
- "What are your thoughts about the interface design?"

---

## Use Standardised Questionnaires

Standardised questionnaires provide **consistency and validity**:

| Questionnaire | Focus | License |
|---------------|-------|---------|
| **QUIS** (Questionnaire for User Interaction Satisfaction) | User satisfaction | Licensed |
| **SUMI** (Software Usability Measurement Inventory) | Software usability | Licensed |
| **PSSUQ** (Post-Study System Usability Questionnaire) | System usability post-task | Licensed |
| **SUS** (System Usability Scale) | General usability | Free (cite source) |

**Other UX aspects to measure:**
- Affective responses (emotional reactions)
- Agent believability
- Check literature (e.g., GODSPEED scale for robot interaction)

**Recommendation:** Use validated questionnaires rather than custom questions when possible

---

## Ethics of Experiments (Overview)

Key ethical considerations:

- **MACS Ethics Clearance Process** – Must be completed
- **Information Sheet and Informed Consent** – Must be provided to participants
- **Data Confidentiality and Privacy** – Must be protected
- **Anonymisation vs. Pseudo-anonymisation** – Choose appropriate approach
- **Common Errors** – Understand what to avoid

**Always:** Ask your supervisor if in doubt.

---

## What is Ethics?

**Definition:** Ethics studies right and wrong and how we should act when actions affect others.

**Computer Ethics:** Deals with ethical issues in use, design, management of IT and policies.

**Key Point:** Ethics is part of the design process, not an afterthought.

**Reference:** [sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/computer-ethics](https://sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/computer-ethics)

---

## Why is Ethics Important?

- **Moral and societal duty** – Responsibility to protect participants
- **Duty of care** – To participants and their data
- **Avoid harm** – Misrepresentation, discomfort, bias, conflicts of interest
- **Compliance** – Legal requirement under GDPR and other regulations

**⚠️ CRITICAL:** Do not start the project without ethical approval; starting without approval may be **academic misconduct**.

## Vulnerable Participants (Detailed)

### Who is Vulnerable?

- **Children** – Limited ability to give informed consent
- **Elderly** – May have reduced capacity
- **Mentally ill** – May lack understanding
- **People with disabilities** – May need additional support
- **Contextually vulnerable** – Unemployed, migrants, refugees, prisoners

### Protection Measures

#### For Children

- **Parental/Guardian Consent:** Must obtain written permission from legal guardians
- **Legal Clearance:** May require Disclosure Scotland (Edinburgh) or equivalent authority
- **Child-Appropriate Materials:** Use simple language, appropriate format
- **Assent from Child:** Child should also agree, where developmentally appropriate

#### General Vulnerable Participant Protections

**Design must account for vulnerabilities:**

- Provide **"STOP" cards** – Allow participant to signal distress/overstress and pause/exit
- **Simple consent materials** – Easy-to-understand language and visual aids
- **Extra care in recruitment** – Ensure voluntary participation, no coercion
- **Shortened sessions** – Reduce burden/fatigue
- **Support available** – Provide contact for concerns/distress post-study

#### Disclosure/Background Checks

## Informed Consent Process

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Identify target population** – Who will participate?
2. **Produce documents** – Information Sheet & Consent Form
3. **Present and discuss** – Explain study to prospective participants
4. **Allow thinking time** – No pressure to immediate decision
5. **Answer questions** – Give full opportunity for clarification
6. **Obtain signed consent** – Get written/documented agreement
7. **Store securely** – Keep consent forms with restricted access
8. **Upload for evidence** – Provide to University records post-study

### Information Sheet

**Must include:**

- **No pressure to participate** – Clearly voluntary
- **Study aim** – Clear, non-technical explanation
- **Process** – What will the participant do?
- **Risks** – Any potential harm or discomfort
- **Why needed** – Justification for research
- **Data collection** – What information will be gathered?
- **Data storage** – Secure University servers (e.g., OneDrive)
- **Reporting** – How and to whom will findings be reported?
- **Confidentiality** – How will privacy be protected?
- **Right to withdraw** – Can participant discontinue anytime?
- **Contact details** – Researcher and supervisor contact information

### Consent Form

**Must include:**

- **Study description** – Objective overview
- **Tasks involved** – What participant will do step-by-step
- **Data security** – Where data will be stored (secured University server)
- **Right to contact researcher** – To request data removal
- **Voluntary participation** – Participation is voluntary
- **Right to withdraw** – Can withdraw at any time without penalty
- **Signature** – Dated participant signature

**Additional for sensitive studies:**

- Screening questions to identify vulnerable users
- Clear statements about any discomfort/risk
- Debriefing information (for deception studies)

**Example forms available on Canvas.**

## Data Confidentiality & GDPR

### Key Questions to Address

- **Who has access** to collected data? (e.g., student, supervisor, no others)
- **What if data leaked?** (risk assessment)
- **GDPR compliance** – Confidentiality and integrity are fundamental

### Data Management

- **Anonymise data** – Remove all identifying information where possible
- **Store securely** – Use University server (e.g., OneDrive) with password protection
- **Restrict access** – Limit to authorized persons only
- **Video/Audio recording** – Requires explicit consent and inclusion in consent form
- **Retention period** – Specify how long data will be kept (typically 3-5 years minimum)

### GDPR Principles

Under GDPR, personal data must be:
- **Confidential** – Protected from unauthorized access
- **Integral** – Accurate and not altered
- **Encrypted** – When stored or transmitted
- **Retained** – Only as long as necessary

## Personal Data: Anonymisation vs. Pseudo-anonymisation

### Fully Anonymised Data

**Definition:** Irreversible removal of personal identifiers so data subject is no longer identifiable.

**Characteristics:**
- No link to identity
- Cannot identify the person
- Falls **outside** definition of personal data under GDPR
- No consent required; no GDPR limitations

**What to avoid in anonymised data:**
- Names, addresses, phone numbers, emails
- Full postcodes, Social Security numbers
- Photos/identifying images
- Date of birth (especially with other identifiers)
- Any field that could identify the person

**Advantage:** Ensures confidentiality

**Disadvantage:** Cannot do follow-up or verification

### Pseudo-anonymised (Linked/Coded) Data

**Definition:** Personal data processed such that it cannot be attributed to a specific data subject without additional information kept separately.

**Characteristics:**
- Uses random ID codes (e.g., Subject7, P001)
- Mapping of ID to identity kept in separate **encrypted file**
- Re-identifiable if key is compromised
- Still considered **personal data** under GDPR

**Implementation:**
- Collect personal data if needed for follow-up
- Assign participant unique ID in main dataset
- Store ID-to-identity mapping in separate encrypted file
- Keep mapping file secure and access-restricted

**Advantage:** Allows follow-up, verification, providing feedback to participants

**Disadvantage:** Requires secure key management; still counts as personal data processing

### Recommendation

**Choose based on your needs:**
- Use **fully anonymised** for maximum privacy protection (no follow-up needed)
- Use **pseudo-anonymised** if you need to contact participants later or verify data

**Must state your approach in Ethics Form.**

## Practical Guidance: Anonymise/Pseudo-anonymise

### Anonymisation (Fully Unlinked)

**Process:**
1. Collect **no personal data** (no names, emails, addresses, etc.)
2. Avoid any field or combination that could identify a user
3. Use generic identifiers (e.g., "Participant 1", "User A")
4. Ensure data cannot be cross-referenced with external sources

**Checklist:**
- ✅ No names
- ✅ No email addresses
- ✅ No phone numbers
- ✅ No full postal codes
- ✅ No dates of birth (or age only, with caution)
- ✅ No photographs/video that shows face
- ✅ No job titles or identifiable work details
- ✅ No account IDs/usernames
- ✅ No biometric data

### Pseudo-anonymisation (Linked/Coded)

**Process:**
1. Collect personal data **if needed for follow-up** (e.g., contact email)
2. **In main dataset:** Use participant ID codes (e.g., "P001", "S42")
3. **In separate file:** Create mapping: P001 → john.smith@example.com
4. **Store separately:** Keep mapping file in encrypted, password-protected location
5. **Access control:** Only authorized persons access the mapping file
6. **Data destruction:** Explain in consent how and when mapping will be deleted

**Implementation Example:**
```
Main Dataset (data_study.xlsx):
P001, Good, 4.2, 87
P002, Excellent, 4.8, 92

Mapping File (mapping_encrypted.xlsx) [KEEP SEPARATE, ENCRYPTED]:
P001, john.smith@example.com
P002, jane.doe@example.com
```

**Participant Rights:**
- Participants have right to access their data
- Participants have right to request data deletion
- Include researcher contact in consent form for these requests

## Deception / Wizard of Oz

### When Deception is Used

Some studies require **deception** (e.g., Wizard of Oz paradigm where human appears to be an AI system).

### Ethical Requirements

- **Justify necessity** – Explain in Ethics Form why deception is essential
- **Reveal deception** – Always reveal the deception to participants at study end
- **Include all withdrawals** – Even participants who withdraw early must be informed of the deception
- **Debrief thoroughly** – Answer questions about the deception

### Example: Wizard of Oz Study

- **Study claim:** "Testing a conversational agent's helpfulness"
- **Reality:** A human operator is behind the scenes
- **Justification:** Revealing the AI identity would bias responses
- **Debrief:** At study end, reveal the deception and explain why

## Health and Safety Risk Assessment

### When Required

Consider health & safety when your study uses:

- **Non-standard devices** – Mobile phones, tablets, wearables, robots, VR/AR headsets
- **Physical interaction** – Robot interaction, haptic feedback
- **Visual/sensory strain** – Eye-strain, cyber-sickness with VR/AR
- **Psychological stress** – Emotionally charged content, serious games
- **Medical devices** – Any medical equipment or medicines

### Risk Categories

| Category | Examples | Considerations |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Physical risks** | Robot interaction, haptic feedback, falls | Injury likelihood and severity |
| **Sensory risks** | Eye-strain, cyber-sickness, hearing damage | Duration, intensity, user susceptibility |
| **Psychological risks** | Stress, anxiety, emotional disturbance | Content sensitivity, user vulnerabilities |
| **Privacy risks** | Sensor data collection, location tracking | Data sensitivity, unauthorized access |

### Mitigation Strategies

- **Equipment inspection** – Ensure devices are safe
- **Time limits** – Limit exposure to prevent strain
- **Breaks** – Schedule regular breaks
- **Screening** – Ask about health conditions (e.g., motion sickness for VR)
- **Support available** – Have supervisor/support person present
- **Debrief** – Check participant well-being post-study

### Mobile Devices Note

**⚠️ Important:** Mobile devices (phones, tablets, wearables) are **NOT standard devices** for ethics approval. Cannot predict usage circumstances. Must conduct full risk assessment.

## Ethically Concerning Studies

### Topics Requiring Special Care

Studies involving:

- **Health-related topics** – Medical conditions, treatments, side effects
- **Emotional state** – Mental health, trauma, sensitive experiences
- **Sensitive subjects** – Politics, religion, controversial issues

### Required Approach

**Must demonstrate:**

1. **Consideration of negative effects** – What could go wrong?
2. **Mitigation strategies** – How will you minimize harm?
3. **Support availability** – What resources are available if participant is distressed?

**Examples of mitigation:**

- Avoid causing panic or undue stress
- Provide mental health resources/contacts
- Allow easy exit from study
- Brief participant about content beforehand
- Have support person available
- Include debrief with well-being check

## Conversational Agents

### Ethical Considerations

Conversational agents (chatbots, virtual assistants, AI assistants) present unique risks:

- **Unpredictable behavior** – AI may behave unexpectedly
- **Sensitive information disclosure** – Participants may reveal private information
- **Emotional engagement** – Users may develop inappropriate relationships
- **Bias in responses** – LLM-based systems may produce biased outputs

### Risk Assessment for Your Study

**Address in Ethics Form:**

- What topics can the agent discuss?
- How will you monitor/control agent responses?
- What happens if the agent produces harmful content?
- How will you mitigate risks of sensitive information disclosure?
- Will you use real LLM or constrained/scripted agent?
- What safeguards are in place?

### Special Concern Areas

If your agent handles:
- **Mental health topics** – Requires special care; not substitute for professional help
- **Medical information** – Cannot provide medical advice
- **Crisis situations** – Must have emergency resources available
- **Sensitive domains** – Must have content controls and override options

## External Datasets and Algorithms

### Ethical Considerations for External Data

When using publicly available datasets:

- **Provenance** – Verify where data came from and its ethical clearance
- **Public availability** – Confirm data was intended for public research use
- **Anonymisation status** – Was data anonymised at source?
- **Legal terms** – Check licensing and terms of use
- **Secondary use** – Is your use consistent with original purpose?

### Algorithms and AI Models

When using algorithms (AI, deep learning, LLMs):

- **Possible biases** – AI systems can perpetuate historical biases
- **Responsibility** – You are responsible for algorithm outputs
- **Transparency** – Explain how algorithm works in study description

### Examples of Problematic Systems

- **Google Photo Classification** – Misidentified photos of people; had racial bias
- **Microsoft Tay Chatbot** – Adopted inappropriate/offensive language from social media
- **Facial Recognition** – Accurate for some demographics, highly biased for others

**Lesson:** Always validate algorithms on your population and report any limitations.
